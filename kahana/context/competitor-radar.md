## Island Browser

**One-line summary:** Enterprise browser platform built for security, governance, and SaaS access control. CISO-led sale.
**Market position:** Leader in enterprise browser security
**Pricing:** Enterprise contract, estimated $40–$80/seat/year
**Typical buyer:** CISO, Head of Security, IT Security

**Their strengths:**
- Purpose-built for zero-trust and DLP (data loss prevention)
- Deep integration with enterprise identity (Okta, Azure AD)
- Strong brand in security-conscious enterprises and financial services
- Chromium base = Chrome extension compatibility

**Their weaknesses:**
- Deployed by IT/Security, not chosen by end users — low adoption buy-in
- Zero AI productivity features; no natural language interface
- Productivty is not a design goal — flow state and knowledge worker UX are ignored
- Requires IT-led deployment; no PLG path; no individual trial

**Why accounts switch to / choose us:**
- When the primary driver is team productivity, not security compliance
- When ESAs or engineers want a browser they actually chose, not one IT mandated
- When the team is frustrated with Chrome's limitations but doesn't need Island's governance overhead

**How to handle the Island objection:**
> "Island is the right answer if your primary requirement is DLP and zero-trust browser security — they're excellent at that. What we solve is a different problem: the cognitive load and context switching that's costing your Solutions team 30+ minutes per day. Those two products can coexist — Island for governed access, Oasis for the knowledge workers who need to stay in flow. The question is which problem you're trying to solve first."

**Signal that an account uses Island:**
- Job postings mentioning "Island Browser" or "enterprise browser security"
- CISO or Security team leading browser vendor evaluation
- Financial services or healthcare company (Island's primary verticals)

**Competitive message for outreach:**
> "Most browser conversations at companies like yours start in Security. This one's different — it starts with your ESA team and the 3 browser windows, 40 tabs, and 6 context switches per hour that are costing them the focus they need to do their best work."

## Arc Browser

**One-line summary:** Consumer productivity browser with strong design aesthetic and word-of-mouth growth. No enterprise tier.
**Market position:** Challenger in consumer productivity browsers
**Pricing:** Free (consumer)
**Typical buyer:** Individual knowledge worker, developer, designer — bottom-up, no enterprise sale

**Their strengths:**
- Exceptional UI/UX — the best-looking browser on the market
- Strong developer and designer community
- Spaces and profiles UI reduces tab clutter for individual users
- Strong word-of-mouth and social media presence

**Their weaknesses:**
- No enterprise deployment, no team management, no admin controls
- No voice control, no natural language commands
- Chromium base = Google data layer
- No B2B pricing or procurement path
- iOS/macOS only — no Windows support (limiting for enterprise)

**Why accounts switch to us:**
- When teams need enterprise management on top of individual productivity
- When Windows users can't use Arc at all
- When the team wants voice control and AI command interface, not just better UI

**How to handle the Arc objection:**
> "Arc is excellent for individual use — if you're on macOS and picking your own browser, it's a strong choice. What we do is different: Oasis works on Windows and Mac, deploys centrally, and the interface is natural language — you don't navigate menus to manage your browser, you just tell it what to do. If 5+ people on your team are Arc users, that tells me you already have a browser productivity problem — Oasis is the enterprise version of that answer."

**Signal that an account uses Arc:**
- Individual LinkedIn posts from ESAs or engineers praising Arc
- Job descriptions mentioning Arc Browser
- macOS-heavy teams with design/dev culture

## Chrome Enterprise / Google Chrome

**One-line summary:** The default enterprise browser; wins through Google Workspace integration and ubiquity.
**Market position:** Dominant incumbent
**Pricing:** Free (Chrome); Chrome Enterprise Plus ~$6/user/month for managed features
**Typical buyer:** IT, backed by default organizational inertia

**Their strengths:**
- Zero deployment friction — already installed everywhere
- Deep Google Workspace integration (Docs, Meet, Gmail, Drive)
- Massive extension ecosystem
- IT admin tooling for enterprise fleet management

**Their weaknesses:**
- Privacy: extensive Google telemetry by default; browsing data used in ad targeting
- Zero AI productivity features (as of 2025)
- No natural language interface; no voice control
- UI unchanged for years — no meaningful UX innovation
- Users don't choose Chrome; they're defaulted into it

**Why accounts switch to us:**
- When privacy matters (Firefox base = no Google data layer)
- When team wants AI-powered workflow acceleration, not just a managed browser
- When ESAs and knowledge workers want a browser they actively like using

**How to handle the Chrome objection:**
> "Chrome is fine as the default — nobody gets fired for staying on Chrome. But there's a difference between the browser IT deploys and the browser people want to use. Oasis sits on top of your existing setup — it's the layer that makes your ESA team's browser intelligent. The question isn't Chrome vs. Oasis; it's whether your knowledge workers are spending 20 minutes a day on browser management that they shouldn't have to."

**Signal that an account uses Chrome Enterprise:**
- IT job postings mentioning "Chrome Enterprise" or "Google Workspace"
- G Suite / Google Workspace as the company's collaboration stack (obvious from job postings)
- Security-first browser RFPs

## Here Browser

**One-line summary:** Enterprise browser focused on "toggle tax" reduction through workflow automation and context management.
**Market position:** Niche challenger in enterprise productivity browsers
**Pricing:** Enterprise (pricing not public)
**Typical buyer:** IT, Operations, or Productivity leads at workflow-heavy enterprises

**Their strengths:**
- Designed specifically for reducing context switching in complex org workflows
- Workflow automation layer — connects apps, not just tabs
- Enterprise-first positioning (no consumer tier)
- Strong messaging around "toggle tax" (overlaps with our language)

**Their weaknesses:**
- No natural language interface; no voice control
- Complex setup and deployment — not PLG-friendly
- No B2C self-serve path; requires IT engagement from day one
- Less focused on knowledge workers as individuals; more on enterprise process flows

**Why accounts switch to us:**
- When individual knowledge worker adoption matters (our PLG path wins)
- When voice control and natural language command are the desired UX
- When the team wants frictionless individual onboarding before enterprise expansion

**How to handle the Here objection:**
> "Here is good if you need workflow automation across apps — it's a layer above the browser. Oasis is different: we've rebuilt the browser itself so you interact with it through language. Here requires an IT project to implement workflows; Oasis requires nothing — users install it, say what they want, and are productive immediately. For your ESA team specifically, the voice control piece is where the real time saving happens."

## SigmaOS

**One-line summary:** Consumer AI browser with WebKit base; "Airis" AI assistant; workspaces and vertical tabs.
**Market position:** Niche consumer AI browser
**Pricing:** Free + Pro $20/month (same as Oasis)
**Typical buyer:** Individual knowledge worker, usually developer or creative

**Their strengths:**
- Clean minimal design
- AI assistant (Airis) that can interact with page content
- Workspaces feature
- Same price point as Oasis ($20/mo)

**Their weaknesses:**
- WebKit base (Safari engine) — limited extension support vs. Firefox
- No enterprise offering; no team management; no admin controls
- No voice control
- No B2B deployment path

**Competitive message:**
> "SigmaOS is the closest consumer analog to what we're building — AI-assisted browser, workspaces, same price. The difference: we're built on Firefox (not WebKit), we have voice control, and we're building enterprise management for teams. If you're looking at SigmaOS for your team, you've already identified the right problem — Oasis is the version built for a company deployment."

## Zen Browser

**One-line summary:** Open-source, beautifully designed Firefox-based browser with workspaces, compact mode, and split view. Free.
**Market position:** Niche consumer privacy browser
**Pricing:** Free (open source)
**Typical buyer:** Privacy-focused individual developer or power user

**Their strengths:**
- Free and open source — zero cost objection
- Firefox base (same as us) — appeals to privacy-focused users
- Genuinely good UX: compact mode, split view, workspaces
- Active community

**Their weaknesses:**
- No AI features, no natural language commands, no voice control
- No enterprise offering — community project, no SLA
- No team management or admin features
- No commercial support

**Competitive message:**
> "Zen is a great personal browser if you want privacy and good tab management for free. What it doesn't have: any AI. There's no natural language interface, no voice control, no intelligence applied to your work. Oasis takes the Firefox foundation Zen is built on and adds the AI layer that actually eliminates the manual work."
