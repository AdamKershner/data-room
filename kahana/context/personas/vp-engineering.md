# Persona: VP of Engineering / Director of Engineering

*Economic buyer and executive sponsor. Approves the budget, cares about team productivity at scale. Doesn't use Oasis themselves (usually), but sees the compound value across the engineering org.*

---

## Overview

**Title(s):** VP of Engineering, Director of Engineering, Head of Engineering, CTO (at smaller startups <100 employees), VP of Platform Engineering
**Typical seniority:** Director to C-Suite
**Decision role:** Economic buyer — approves budget and procurement, often not involved in early evaluation. Enters the deal when their team's champion escalates it.
**Found at companies:** B2B SaaS startups (50–500 employees); they exist at Series A and above; below that the CTO fills this role.

---

## What They Care About

**Their primary metric:** Team velocity, engineering productivity, developer experience (DX) score, and org health. They're measured on shipping and retention — both code and people.

**Their biggest problem right now:**
Their team is producing well but they know there's friction they can't see. They see Jira tickets moving, but they know their senior engineers are constantly context-switching. They've heard complaints about tab overload and browser setup being "distracting." They've probably read something about cognitive load and developer productivity. They're looking for tools that give their team compound productivity gains — things that save 15 minutes a day per person, multiplied by 20 engineers.

They care about:
- **Retention signal:** Engineers who have great tooling stay longer. A VP of Engineering who invests in their team's workflow is signaling they take DX seriously.
- **Onboarding efficiency:** New engineers spend their first weeks drowning in browser chaos. Anything that cuts onboarding-to-productive by even a few days matters.
- **Standardization:** A consistent browser environment means fewer "works on my machine" problems and clearer screen sharing/collaboration.

**What a good week looks like:** Team shipped on time. No blockers they couldn't see coming. Senior engineers are in deep work, not firefighting. New hire got productive faster than expected.

**What a bad week looks like:** Two senior engineers complained about tool sprawl. Retro surfaced "too many context switches" as a team complaint. They asked IT to evaluate 3 productivity tools and none got deployed.

## How They Buy

**Involvement in purchase:** Approves, but rarely initiates. Their ESA or senior engineer champion brings Oasis to them after personal validation. The VP of Eng's job is to evaluate: is this worth the money? Will the team actually use it? Is there a security concern?
**Discovery behavior:** They hear about tools from their team, from other VPs at conferences or Slack communities, from CTO newsletters. They don't install trial software themselves.
**Evaluation style:** ROI-framed. They want to understand the cost per seat, the expected time savings, and whether IT can deploy without a project. They'll ask their champion to run a 2-week pilot and report back.

**Common objections:**
- "How is this different from just buying Arc or using better Chrome shortcuts?" → "Arc is a personal choice for individual Mac users — no enterprise deployment, no Windows support, no admin visibility. Oasis is a company-managed deployment with centralized control and a team tier. The difference is shipping a consistent environment vs. hoping each person figures it out individually."
- "What's the ROI?" → "If each engineer on your team saves 20 minutes/day of browser management, that's 1.5+ hours/week per person. At 20 engineers, that's 30 hours/week — roughly 1 full-time contributor's output, every week. The question is whether that math holds up in your team's specific workflow, which is why we do a pilot first."
- "IT needs to be involved for any browser change" → "We support Firefox enterprise deployment — MDM, Group Policy, centralized configuration. Give me 30 minutes with your IT lead and I can walk through the deployment path."

## How to Reach Them

**Best channel:** Email. They're not active on LinkedIn for cold outreach. Events (engineering leadership summits, CTO breakfasts) are high-quality touch points.
**Best time:** They read email in the morning (7–9am) and last thing at day (5–6pm). Avoid during business hours — they're in meetings.
**What gets their attention:**
- Team-level framing, not individual productivity — "20 engineers × 20 minutes/day" resonates more than "you'll save time"
- Specific, concrete numbers — they're engineers, they want data
- Short and confident — they don't have time for a long pitch; respect their time

**What gets ignored:**
- "I wanted to reach out about improving your team's productivity" — too generic
- Feature lists — they don't evaluate features, their team does
- LinkedIn InMail — too much noise there

---

## Message Framework

**Value prop for this persona:**
Every engineer on your team loses 15–30 minutes per day to browser context switching. Oasis converts that overhead to focus time — compounding at scale. The productivity gain that looks small per person becomes significant across an org.

**Proof points that resonate:**
- Compound time math: X engineers × Y min/day × 5 days = Z hours/week recovered
- Onboarding acceleration: new engineers are productive faster when browser management is handled
- [After pilot data]: "A [similar size] engineering team saw X% reduction in context-switch time during 30-day pilot"

## Sample Outreach Hooks

*Only reach this persona when: (a) ESA champion already validated, or (b) 3+ users from company domain active, or (c) the company is Tier 1 and a warm intro is possible.*

**Hook — Champion already using Oasis (multi-user signal):**
> "[First name] — [3/4/5] people from your team have been using Oasis on their own. Before it spreads further without a managed setup, worth a conversation? 20 engineers each saving 20 minutes/day adds up fast. Happy to send a one-pager first if that's easier."

**Hook — Series A/B + ESA team exists:**
> "[First name] — as [Company] scales post-[Series A/B], the browser chaos problem compounds with every new hire. ESAs and senior engineers are the most expensive people to have doing manual window management. We've built something that eliminates that overhead — 15 minutes to show you how?"
