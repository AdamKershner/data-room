# Persona: Enterprise Solutions Architect (ESA)

*Primary champion persona. This is who Oasis was built for — and the person most likely to drive internal adoption and advocate for the enterprise deal.*

---

## Overview

**Title(s):** Enterprise Solutions Architect, Senior Solutions Architect, Solutions Architect, Principal Solutions Architect, Solutions Engineer, Sr. Solutions Engineer
**Typical seniority:** Senior IC to Staff — often reports to VP of Solutions Engineering or VP of Sales Engineering
**Decision role:** Champion — identifies the pain, validates the product, advocates internally for the purchase
**Found at companies:** B2B SaaS companies (50–500 employees) with a dedicated Solutions or Pre-Sales Engineering function

---

## What They Care About

**Their primary metric:** Customer outcomes and deal technical win rates — they succeed when the solution they design actually gets implemented and the customer succeeds with it.

**Their biggest problem right now:**
They live in 4–8 browser contexts simultaneously. A typical day includes: client documentation in one window, internal product wiki in another, sandbox environments in a third, Salesforce and Slack in background tabs, competitor research somewhere, and the actual customer call on a second screen. Every context switch — switching from one client's environment to another, back to internal docs, then to Slack — costs mental overhead they can't afford when they're deep in technical problem-solving.

They lose 20–40 minutes a day to manual browser management they don't think of as "management" — they just know that their workflow feels fragmented, they spend too much time hunting for tabs, and they can't get into deep work when every other minute something pulls their attention.

**What a good week looks like:** They close 3 technical discovery calls and come out of each knowing exactly what to propose. They have a clean architecture draft ready for a Tier 1 prospect. They're not losing track of context between clients.

**What a bad week looks like:** They're hunting for that one tab with the customer's requirements doc they know they had open yesterday. They rebuild the same research twice. They're making small errors because they're carrying too much context in their heads. They can feel themselves operating at 70% because the environment is fighting them.

---

## How They Buy

**Involvement in purchase:** Initiates and champions. They find Oasis through PLG (install it themselves, or a colleague shares it). Once they see value, they're the internal advocate.
**Discovery behavior:** Word-of-mouth from peers, Twitter/LinkedIn, Product Hunt, Hacker News — they try new developer/productivity tools constantly. They don't wait for IT to evaluate tools; they install first, ask IT later.
**Evaluation style:** Hands-on. They try the product personally for 2–3 weeks before making any case internally. They'll hit the paywall and that's when the enterprise conversation starts.
**Common objections:**
- "I love it but I'd need to get IT approval to deploy it for the team" → "Firefox enterprise deployment is supported natively. Your IT team has managed Firefox before — give me 20 minutes with them and I can walk them through the setup."
- "I'm not sure the rest of the team will adopt it" → "You've already proven the value to yourself. The voice control thing is what usually converts skeptics — once someone tries saying 'open tabs 3 and 4 in split view' instead of dragging windows, they don't go back."
- "We already have Chrome Enterprise" → "Chrome Enterprise is IT's browser. Oasis is your browser. They're solving different problems — IT uses Chrome for policy; you'd use Oasis for the 6 hours a day of actual knowledge work."

---

## How to Reach Them

**Best channel:** Email first, LinkedIn second. They respond to email if the first line is specific and relevant.
**Best time:** Tuesday–Thursday, 8–10am local. They're in deep work by 10am and won't see cold outreach until the next morning.
**What gets their attention:**
- Specific mention of the exact pain (tab sprawl, multi-client context switching, split-view workflows)
- A concrete command example — "open tabs 3 and 4 in split view" lands harder than "reduce context switching"
- Peer reference — "We're working with Solutions teams at [similar company type]"
- Very short first email — they read and delete at high speed; under 100 words gets read

**What gets ignored:**
- "I wanted to reach out because..." openers
- Generic productivity claims without specifics
- Long emails with multiple paragraphs
- Marketing language about "AI-powered workflows"

## Message Framework

**Value prop for this persona:**
Oasis eliminates the browser management tax that costs Solutions Architects 20–40 minutes per day — so the time and focus that was going to window dragging goes back to actual work.

**Proof points that resonate:**
- The specific voice command examples — "close all tabs except this one," "open client research and product docs in split view" — land better than any metric
- [After pilot data]: "A Solutions team at [company type] reduced browser context-switching overhead by X minutes/day during their pilot"
