# Playbook: Competitor Switch

*How to handle accounts evaluating or using a competing browser. Oasis's competitive landscape has two distinct categories: enterprise security browsers (Island, Chrome Enterprise) and consumer productivity browsers (Arc, SigmaOS, Zen). The playbook is different for each.*

---

## Trigger

One of the following:
- Account's tech stack shows an existing browser deployment (Island, Chrome Enterprise, Here)
- Prospect mentions a competitor in a reply or discovery call
- Warmly or Clay reveals a competitor evaluation in progress (job posting for "enterprise browser evaluation")
- G2 or review intent signal from a competitor's page

---

## Step 1: Identify the Competitive Situation

Not all competitive situations are the same.

**Category A: Enterprise security browser (Island, Chrome Enterprise, LayerX, CyberArk Secure Browser)**
They bought for security/governance. Oasis does not displace this — it complements it. Frame as "two browsers for two jobs."

**Category B: Consumer productivity browser (Arc, SigmaOS, Zen, Workona)**
Individual users chose this for themselves. No enterprise deployment, no B2B budget. Frame as "you've already identified the problem — Oasis is the enterprise version of the answer."

**Category C: Default incumbent (Chrome, Firefox, Edge — no active browser product)**
Not a competitive displacement. They're not "choosing" a browser — they're on whatever was already there. Oasis enters as additive, not replacement.

**Category D: Active evaluation of a competitor**
They're running an RFP or structured evaluation that includes other browser products. Time-sensitive.

---

## Category A: Enterprise Security Browser (Island, Chrome Enterprise)

**Approach:** Never position as a replacement. Position as complementary.

The CISO bought Island for DLP and zero-trust. The VP of Engineering wants their ESA team to have Oasis for productivity. These are not in conflict — until you make them conflict by pitching "switch from Island."

```
Read context/competitor-radar.md for the [Island / Chrome Enterprise] battlecard.
Research [company.com] to understand their org structure.
Write a first touch for the [VP Engineering / ESA] persona (NOT the CISO or IT lead).
The frame: Island handles governance, Oasis handles the knowledge worker experience.
These coexist. Reference context/personas/vp-engineering.md for tone.
```

**What not to do:** Don't pitch to the CISO or IT lead first — they already have their browser solution. Go around them, to the business team.

**Handling the IT blocker:**
> "I understand Chrome Enterprise is your managed deployment. Oasis doesn't replace that — IT keeps Chrome as the policy browser. Oasis is an additional choice for your Solutions and Engineering teams who need a productivity-focused environment for their actual knowledge work. Two browsers, two purposes."

---

## Category B: Consumer Productivity Browser (Arc, SigmaOS, Zen)

**Approach:** Affirm their taste. Elevate to enterprise.

An ESA using Arc has already identified the right problem. They're the perfect Oasis candidate — they just haven't had a reason to pay $20/mo or consider team deployment.

```
Read context/competitor-radar.md for the [Arc / SigmaOS] battlecard.
Write a first touch for an ESA at [company] who is known to use/advocate for [competitor].
Frame: they've already validated the productivity browser category.
Oasis adds what [competitor] doesn't have: voice control, enterprise deployment, Firefox privacy base.
Reference context/personas/enterprise-solutions-architect.md.
```

**Hook that works:**
> "Saw you're a [Arc/SigmaOS] user — means you've already felt the tab-overload problem. Oasis does something those browsers don't: you just tell it what you want out loud. 'Open tabs 3 and 4 in split view' and it happens. Happy to show you in 10 minutes."

**What not to do:** Don't dismiss Arc or SigmaOS — they have genuine fans. Acknowledge the choice, differentiate on voice control and enterprise features.

---

## Category C: No Active Browser Product (Incumbents)

**Approach:** Don't frame as competitive. Frame as additive.

Chrome/Firefox/Edge aren't competitors in a real sense — they're defaults. No one actively chose Chrome; IT just never changed the standard. Oasis enters as a productivity layer that individual users adopt voluntarily, not as a replacement for an IT decision.

This is actually the majority of your market. Most outreach doesn't require a competitive frame at all.

---

## Category D: Active Evaluation — Comparing to Competitor

**Immediate action. Do this within 24 hours of learning about the evaluation.**

### Step 1: Understand the Evaluation Criteria

What are they actually evaluating on? Security? AI features? Ease of deployment? Find this out before building your comparison.

```
What did the prospect say about their evaluation criteria?
What dimensions matter most to [persona]?
Pull the relevant battlecards from context/competitor-radar.md.
```

### Step 2: Build a Targeted Comparison

Don't build a generic feature matrix. Build a comparison that addresses their stated criteria.

```
Read context/competitor-radar.md.
Build a comparison of Oasis vs. [Competitor] focused specifically on [criteria they named].
Format as a short one-pager they can share internally — not a sales deck.
```

### Step 3: Equip Your Champion

Your champion needs language to sell internally. Give them:
- What to say when IT asks about the competing product
- Why Oasis is the right choice for their specific team's workflow (not generic)
- A reference scenario that mirrors their situation

```
Read context/personas/enterprise-solutions-architect.md.
Write internal talking points for [champion name] to use when presenting Oasis to [VP Eng / IT].
Frame: why natural language control is the right choice for their [ESA / solutions] team specifically.
Keep it to 5 bullet points — they need to remember it in a meeting, not read a document.
```

---

## Competitive Intelligence — Keep Battlecards Current

After every competitive win or loss, update `context/competitor-radar.md`:

- **Win:** What was the deciding factor? What did the champion say closed it?
- **Loss:** What did the competitor offer that we couldn't match? Was it features, security posture, price, or relationships?
- **Pattern:** After 3+ wins/losses against the same competitor, document the pattern

The battlecards must reflect what's happening in actual deals, not what we assume from the outside.
