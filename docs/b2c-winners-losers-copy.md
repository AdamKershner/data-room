## Checklist · Winners and losers intro (three beats)

Winners stop treating Ad Nauseam as background radiation and start treating the swarm as an attack surface. They are the quiet rebels: they notice when the internet feels like a rigged casino, they budget attention like money, they reduce their tracking surface, and they choose tools that do not live on surveillance. Mosquitoes only win when you keep sitting outside without a net.

Losers are not stupid; they stay on default. They tell themselves that is just how the internet works, pay a cognition tax on every task, and leave every switch on. Big-tech mosquitoes sip while they scroll until they are a hollow husk; time stretches thin, then ad after ad; identity splinters until nothing sacred is left to hold for themselves.

Over time, losers feel numbness instead of outrage: the swarm feels inevitable. Winners regain headspace and agency; losers become background to someone else’s optimization story. The fork is not blame; it is whether you act before the default browser finishes selling you out.

## Full framework reference (writer source)

Condensed from `in the context of a narrative story framework with.md`. Use for post drafts, not inline on the checklist.

### Who the winners are

Winners stop treating Ad Nauseam as background radiation and treat it as an **attack surface**. They:

- **Notice the pattern**: stop gaslighting themselves about how “normal” the same shoes, brands, and attention traps feel; recognize a rigged casino when someone designed it that way.
- **Draw a hard line around attention**: blockers, switch browsers, kill third-party cookies, turn off autoplay, unsubscribe ruthlessly; budget and defend attention like money.
- **Reduce surface area**: sign out of default accounts, avoid logging in where unnecessary, use privacy-respecting tools, make the tracking graph sparser on purpose.
- **Build or choose different systems**: adopt products that do not depend on surveillance revenue; pay for tools that respect them.
- **Turn frustration into leverage**: vote with wallets and habits; as builders, make “this sucks” a selling point (quieter feeds, fewer ads, no tracking by default).

**Quiet rebels:** mosquitoes only win when you keep sitting outside without a net.

### What winners get

- Clearer headspace: internet as a tool again, not a trap.
- Truer signals: choices from their priorities, not 40 impressions of wear-down.
- Better long-term leverage: harder to profile and manipulate.
- A sense of agency: “I didn’t just let this happen to me.”

### Who the losers are

Losers are not stupid; they stay on **default**:

- **Normalize the swarm**: “That’s just how the internet works”; annoyance becomes invisible.
- **Leave every switch on**: never review permissions; one big-tech ID everywhere; easiest person to model and monetize.
- **Accept a cognition tax**: banners, pre-rolls, pop-ups, ghost-shoes in the sidebar; slower, more distracted, more exhausted.
- **Get shaped without noticing**: tastes and impulses trained by what is cheap to advertise, not what is good for them.
- **Pay twice**: attention and data first, then money for things they did not need and “premium” to escape the free tier; customers and inventory.

### What happens to losers over time

- **Numbness**: swarm feels inevitable; stop believing anything different is possible.
- **Deeper data trails**: searches, locations, purchases, scrolls constrain what they ever see.
- **Background to someone else’s story**: not villains; raw material. Clicks and pauses train the next generation of targeting.

## Mosquito metaphor + evidence block

**Metaphor (story):** Big-tech mosquitoes drain attention and identity while the user scrolls, visible ads are fatigue; data collection is the sip under the skin.

**Evidence (facts):** Use on loser / “Working for Google” side (`mn-winners-chrome-04`, `mn-ph-chrome-02`). Attribute Surfshark, Forbes, Security.org, [Kahana Chrome 2025 post](https://kahana.co/blog/google-chrome-data-collection-advertising-practices-2025).

- “Chrome is the most data-hungry popular browser, collecting 20–24 different types of personal data, far more than competitors that average just 6.”
- “Chrome is the only major browser that pulls in your phone’s contacts and your payment details, then links that information to your identity and devices.”
- “Google confirms that Chrome has leaked pieces of your browsing history for years via a fundamental browser behavior, now only being locked down in Chrome 136.”
- “Google collects more data than any other big-tech firm, and Chrome-synced browsing history becomes part of the profile that powers its ad and AI systems.”
