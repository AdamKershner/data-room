# Story Framework: Stage guide for content creators

**Purpose:** Teach how to think about each beat in Andy Raskin’s strategic narrative framework, so you can create content that fits **one stage** without mixing beats. This is **pedagogy**, not publishable copy.

**Framework article:** [The Greatest Sales Deck I've Ever Seen](https://medium.com/the-mission/the-greatest-sales-deck-ive-ever-seen-4f4ef3391ba0)

**Checklist:** `/marketing-narrative-checklist` (B2C filter): expand **Learn how to create content for this stage** above each section’s story intro for a short version of this guide.

**Your narrative’s voice:** Use each section’s **story intro** and copy banks on the checklist (e.g. [b2c-narrative-spine.md](../b2c-narrative-spine.md) for the Oasis B2C arc). This guide does not replace that material.

Token: `[[STORY_FRAMEWORK_STAGE_GUIDE]]` → `/b2c-strategic-narrative#story-framework-stages`

---

## How to use this guide

1. Expand the framework panel on the checklist **before** drafting for that section.
2. Keep each post in **one beat**, do not mix change, villain, fork, promised land, gifts, and evidence in one piece.
3. Use **Ideation prompts** to brainstorm; add strong ideas as new checklist tasks with stable IDs.
4. Write publishable copy from your narrative’s story intro and copy banks, not from this guide verbatim.

## Six sections on this checklist (classic five beats)

Andy Raskin’s deck uses **five** beats. This B2C checklist uses **six** sections:

| Stage | Classic beat | What this section teaches |
|-------|--------------|---------------------------|
| Name the change | Name a big change | The world shifted; the old playbook is over |
| Name the villain (extended) | Name the villain | When the arc splits villain work: lived pain first, systemic proof in the next section |
| Winners and losers | Winners and losers | Two futures; stakes without blame |
| Tease the Promised Land | Tease the Promised Land | Imagine relief; don’t hard-sell yet |
| Magic gifts | Magic gifts | One obstacle → one proof |
| Present evidence | Present evidence | Prove the story is true |

## Name a big, relevant change in the world {#section-1-change}

### Andy Raskin beat

**Name a big, relevant change in the world**: why the old game is over.

### What this stage represents

You are not pitching product yet. You name a **shift** the audience already lives inside: industry, behavior, or expectations changed while they were still playing by old rules.

### Audience at this stage

They feel friction but may blame themselves. They need language for “something changed around me” before “something is wrong with me.”

### Effect we want

Recognition and urgency: “the world moved; I’m not crazy.” Validation before prescription.

### Belongs here

- Trend or macro shift posts
- “The old way is over” framing
- Lived pain and metaphor (one proof point max)
- Questions that surface shared experience
- **Real community stories** when available (checklist Name the change: End of Privacy, May 2026) instead of invented vignettes

### Save for later

- Naming the antagonist in depth (villain stage)
- Fork and stakes (winners and losers)
- Future vision (promised land)
- Product features (magic gifts and evidence)

### Ideation prompts

- What changed in your industry that everyone feels but few name?
- When did the old way stop working for you?
- What do people normalize that is actually a symptom of the shift?

## Name the villain {#section-2-villain}

### Andy Raskin beat

**Name the villain**: personify what stands in the way; show motive and scale.

### What this stage represents

The audience needs a **who or what** to blame, not a vague complaint. If your arc used the change stage for *felt* pain, use the villain stage for *systemic* proof: business model, market structure, incentives, data, or policy, with receipts where possible.

### Audience at this stage

Skeptical but engaged. They want evidence, not slogans. They may still think the problem is personal failure.

### Effect we want

Clarity and justified unease about the **system**, not “buy from us today.”

### Belongs here

- Antagonist personified (company, model, habit, status quo)
- Stats and sourced claims
- Motive (“why they act this way”)
- Contrast between lived experience and structural cause

### Save for later

- Winners vs losers fork
- Promised future
- Product proof (magic gifts and evidence)

### Ideation prompts

- Who profits from the status quo?
- What one statistic would make the antagonist undeniable?
- How do you explain motive without sounding like a conspiracy post?

## Show there'll be winners and losers {#section-3-winners-losers}

### Andy Raskin beat

**Show there'll be winners and losers**: two futures; stakes as choice, not shame.

### What this stage represents

After problem and villain, the audience sees a **fork**: people who act vs people who wait. This is loss aversion with empathy, not calling the audience stupid.

### Audience at this stage

Tired but not hopeless. They compare themselves to “people who adapted” vs “people who stayed on default.”

### Effect we want

Agency: “I could choose differently, and waiting has a cost.”

### Belongs here

- Two-path narratives (act vs defer)
- Identity language for each path
- Evidence paired with emotion
- Bridge from problem to “I see myself here”

### Save for later

- Full future vision (promised land)
- Product capabilities (magic gifts)
- Launch proof and brand conclusion (evidence)

### Ideation prompts

- What does “staying on default” cost in a typical week?
- Who is the quiet rebel in your audience?
- What small act of agency could you invite without selling?

## Tease the Promised Land {#section-4-promised-land}

### Andy Raskin beat

**Tease the Promised Land**: help them **imagine** a better future before you prove it.

### What this stage represents

Hope they can picture: calmer workflow, better outcomes, fairer rules. **bounded** promises, not a feature list. Sell the destination, not the vehicle yet.

### Audience at this stage

Burned out but willing to imagine relief. Allergic to vaporware; needs credible, honest boundaries.

### Effect we want

Desire and relief: “I want that future”: without premature product dump.

### Belongs here

- “Imagine…” posts
- Outcome-focused tease (feelings, scenes, principles)
- Credible promises (what you will and won’t claim)
- Contrast with earlier pain without re-litigating the villain

### Save for later

- Feature-by-feature proof (magic gifts)
- Case studies, logos, launch assets (evidence)
- Hard CTAs and pricing (often the evidence stage)

### Ideation prompts

- Finish: “Imagine a world where ___” without naming your product.
- What does success *feel* like in one concrete scene?
- What promise must you qualify to stay honest?

## Introduce features as “magic gifts” {#section-5-magic-gifts}

### Andy Raskin beat

**Introduce features as “magic gifts”**: each gift removes **one obstacle** on the path to the Promised Land.

### What this stage represents

Hope becomes **concrete**. One pain → one capability → old world → new world. Not a roadmap slide; observable contrast.

### Audience at this stage

Asking “what do I actually get?” Comparing to incumbents and defaults.

### Effect we want

Belief the Promised Land is **reachable**: “I can try one step.”

### Belongs here

- One gift per post or asset
- Old world → new world framing
- Honest limits (partial, beta, “fewer not zero”)
- Single clear next step

### Save for later

- Full proof stack (evidence)
- Re-opening macro change (name the change)
- Tease-only imagine posts (promised land)

### Ideation prompts

- Which promised-land promise does this gift prove first?
- What is one before/after the audience can see in 30 seconds?
- Where must you underclaim to stay truthful?

## Present evidence the story is true {#section-6-evidence}

### Andy Raskin beat

**Present evidence the story is true**: proof the Promised Land is real; close the emotional arc.

### What this stage represents

The story earns credibility: customers, data, demos, launch moments, third-party validation. This is **conclusion + proof** after the arc, not where you introduce the problem.

### Audience at this stage

Ready for brand voice, CTAs, and payoff. Some need receipts; others need belonging.

### Effect we want

Trust and calm: “this wasn’t just marketing.”

### Belongs here

- Case studies and user stories
- Screenshots, benchmarks, third-party reports
- Launch and campaign assets
- Emotional brand close (poem, manifesto) **with** proof nearby

### Save for later

- New villain origin stories (change and villain stages)
- Unproven feature claims (magic gifts)
- Tease-only content (promised land)

### Ideation prompts

- What proof would convince *you* after five sections of story?
- Which artifact best closes the loop opened at the start?
- What single CTA matches the emotion of the ending?

## Arc at a glance

| Stage | Emotion | Creator question |
|-------|---------|------------------|
| Name the change | Recognition, urgency | What changed? |
| Name the villain | Clarity, unease | Who or what is the villain? |
| Winners and losers | Stakes, agency | Who wins if they act vs wait? |
| Promised land | Hope, desire | What future can they imagine? |
| Magic gifts | Concrete hope | What one step proves it? |
| Evidence | Trust, calm | What proves it’s real? |
