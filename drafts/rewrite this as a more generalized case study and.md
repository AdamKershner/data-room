## Case Study: Securing SaaS Access for Short‑Term Consultants with a Chromium-Based Enterprise Browser

A mid‑market, SaaS‑first company is looking to provide secure access to its core cloud applications for a growing population of short‑term consultants and third‑party partners. Instead of shipping fully managed laptops, they want a lightweight, managed browser that can be installed on contractors’ existing devices without local admin rights and removed when the engagement ends.

Their key requirements are:

- **Secure access to SaaS apps** for external users, without granting broad device trust or network access.
- A **managed, Chromium-based browser** so that web compatibility and user experience match what employees expect from mainstream browsers like Chrome.[^1][^2]
- **Per-user, per-month licensing**, aligned to flexible and fluctuating contractor headcount.
- Centralized controls for data protection and policy enforcement (e.g., blocking downloads, uploads, or risky actions) applied at the browser/session level rather than through full device management.

By adopting a Chromium-based enterprise browser, this type of company aims to reduce hardware and IT overhead, improve the security posture for third‑party access, and maintain a familiar user experience for consultants.

***

## Market Context in 2026

- Analysts expect **managed and enterprise browsers** (including full browsers and extension‑based models) to see rapid adoption; by around 2026, roughly **25% of enterprises** are projected to be using managed browsers or extensions for security and access control.[^3][^4]
- The **secure / enterprise browser market** is still young but growing quickly, with estimates putting the market in the low‑single‑digit billions of dollars in the mid‑2020s and CAGR estimates in the low‑ to high‑teens over the coming years.[^5][^6]
- Most enterprise browsers are **Chromium‑based**, leveraging Chromium’s dominant share of global browser usage and compatibility with modern SaaS applications.[^7][^2][^1]

From a demand perspective:

- Globally, there are on the order of **tens of thousands of SaaS companies** (roughly 30,000–60,000 depending on estimate) and SaaS is expected to account for over **80–85% of business software** by the mid‑2020s.[^8][^9]
- Organizations now use an average of **100+ SaaS apps**, with large enterprises often using well over 150–400, which increases the need for centralized, browser-level security controls instead of piecemeal per‑app solutions.[^9][^8]

Given these numbers and Gartner‑style forecasts that about **25% of enterprises** will adopt managed/enterprise browsers by 2026, it is reasonable to estimate that **many thousands of companies worldwide**—especially mid‑ to large enterprises with heavy SaaS usage and significant contractor/partner populations—either already are, or soon will be, facing the same “secure SaaS access for external users via a managed Chromium browser” problem that this case study describes.[^4][^3]
